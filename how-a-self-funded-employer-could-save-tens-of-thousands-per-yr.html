Q1: Does Platinum Dermatology Group Charge the Same Amount as Other Providers?

Platinum Dermatology Group's t-test defense (p=0.0725): Platinum Dermatology Group providers present an ordinary two-sample t-test (p=0.0725), claiming "no significant difference." This test assumes the 59 claim amounts from their providers are independent and identically distributed (IID), and the same for the 312 claims from normal providers.

But this assumption is false: Claims from the same provider are correlated (clustered), violating the IID assumption. One provider's patients may be systematically sicker or healthier, leading to higher or lower charges. Probabilistically, if you tell me that the first charge from Dr. Smith is $2,000, an unusually high amount, I now expect Dr. Smith's other charges to be higher than average too. This correlation among claims from the same provider is clustering. Ignoring clustering overestimates variability, inflating significance (lowering p-values). Thus, the t-test p=0.0725 is unreliable.

The correct analysis: The right approach is to use "provider", not "claim", as the unit of analysis. The null hypothesis is the same as for the claims-level t-test - that the average claim amount per provider is the same for Platinum Dermatology Group vs normal derms. The difference comes in how we estimate the variance of the average claim amounts. Rather than looking at claim-to-claim variability (which is artificially high due to clustering), we look at provider-to-provider variability. We do this by bootstrapping (resampling) at the provider level, preserving clustering. The bootstrapping simulates the process of drawing samples by resampling entire providers (with all their 2024 claims) from each group. The standard error of the test statistic (the difference in average claim amounts per provider) is then estimated as the standard deviation of these resampled differences. The p-value from this provider-level test is valid because it correctly accounts for clustering. And the p-value of p=0.0407 detects significant overbilling by Platinum Dermatology Group providers.

The next table shows the results. In it you can see the t-test's inflated standard error of $655/claim, which is what causes its inflated p-value of 0.0725. The provider-level test gives the right answers.

Q2: By How Much Does Platinum Dermatology Group Overcharge?

Now on to the second question - the one that Platinum Dermatology didn't answer.

The data indicates that Platinum Dermatology overcharges by $1,199 per claim ($1,476 for Platinum minus $277 for the other providers). This is the expected value of the difference in average claim charges, and is the best estimate of the amount by which the employer was overcharged. But as with most estimates, there's the best estimate and more conservative versions that take into account uncertainty (the variance of the test statistic).

To do this, we look at the degree to which the test statistic typically deviates from the expected value. Assuming the test statistic (the difference in average claim charges between the two groups) is normally distributed, the test statistic minus 1.645 times its standard error is less than or equal to the expected value 95% of the time. That is,
